THE LITTLE ROBOT THAT COULDN'T
Or, A Blind Assault on the Subject of Robotics
It all started with a simple idea about a robot that couldn’t.

Needybot, in its most basic form, is a robot that needs help to do nearly everything in the physical world. Armed with a very narrow grasp of social psychology, and a few tricks up its shell, Needybot will literally be placed into the social world of Wieden+Kennedy’s Portland office, home to more than 600 people. It’s goal is simple: get help with stuff, and don’t die.

Depending on who you ask, however, Needybot has come to represent many things: an art project, a kind of robotic toddler, a performance art piece, a buddy, a pain in the ass. 

One of the most interesting outcomes of letting Needybot run around our office for the next nine months will be learning what it means to everyone else.

+ + +

Nine months ago we didn’t have any clue what we were getting into. At that time, back in June 2015, only one member of our team had had any robotics experience, which meant the other 25 of us were walking blindly into what has since become one of the most challenging, educational, and fulfilling projects we’ve worked on in the Lodge.

When we didn’t have the technology, we found it. When we didn’t have the skills, we learned, rapidly.

But Needybot is more than just the first robotics project we’ve run at—it is, without contest, the largest-scale exploration into human-computer interaction (HCI) that The Lodge has ever undertaken. In fact, this is quite possibly the largest-scale exploration into HCI any advertising agency has ever undertaken.

Ours has been a road of confusion and frustration, then triumph and pride—we failed, repeatedly, blew the scope (and the team) far out of proportion, and rebooted the project (and the team) more than once.

In between all of this, we kept learning and adapting, and found enough small successes to keep the project ever aimed at its north star. Failure is not an end, but a beginning.

The pace and scale at which we have had to learn in order to produce Needybot has been massive for both our engineering and design teams. You may not think so, but the litany of subjects and skills required to build, shape, and create a fairly useless robot is exhausting.

Simply listing the subjects one can recall at will makes a strong case that Needybot represents a sum of information, knowledge, and craft unlike any other kind of project The Lodge has ever seen:

  • Computer vision
  • 3D-modeling & printing
  • Robotics
  • Sound engineering
  • Interface and interaction design
  • Mapping and localization
  • Illustration
  • Systems programming
  • Machine learning
  • Copywriting
  • Structural engineering
  • Data science
  • Costume fabrication
  • Protocol, upon protocol, upon protocol, upon…


It is a point of pride for The Lodge to be able to say that, apart from the robotics hardware and software platform, we fabricated Needybot 100% in-house. When we didn’t have the technology, we found it. When we didn’t have the skills, we learned, rapidly. Ignorance is not a boundary, but a hurdle. 

We are very sure about one thing, however: Needybot is a kind robot that no one has ever seen before.

Needybot is being launched on a nine-month arc that will see it face new challenges as it integrates with the culture in our office building. Its goals and attitude will definitely hinge upon how the human element of our agency receives and treats it. Although Needybot was created by The Lodge, Needybot itself, along with the people of this agency will be largely responsible for its development as a robot.

It is somewhat fitting that it was nearly 40 weeks ago that we started this crazy project, because, to some of us, Needybot has become a sort of offspring. When we see a face light up during an interaction with Needy, we all feel warm inside. We trade high-fives in the background because we are proud of what we’ve created. We are Needybot’s parents.

Needybot is a labor of love that couldn’t have happened without countless hours of work on the part of many, many talented individuals. Given the spectrum of talent in The Lodge, combined with the cultural background of Wieden+Kennedy, it is arguable that Needybot couldn’t have happened anywhere else.

+ + +

What will happen to Needybot in the coming months? What will it learn about the people at Wieden+Kennedy? What will we learn? Will someone drop-kick Needybot down the stairs? 

We don’t really know the answers to these questions. Well we have some idea, but, then again, we have been gloriously wrong before. We are very sure about one thing, however: Needybot is a kind robot that no one has ever seen before.

Leash Your Cat.

I live in urban Portland, and I want to talk briefly about a really shitty part about living in an urban environment.

My wife and I have a dog. We moved into our neighborhood almost six years ago, and immediately had to build a fence around the yard, so that our dog wouldn't be able to run willy-nilly around the 'hood. This is a given—no one, us included, would even take a second-thought at this.

So, Bailey, our half-sized, perpetually shagrinned, loving dog, is confined to the house or the yard, or she is on a leash. In short, she is a prisoner, but one we take very good care of and love very much.

I understand that some people aren't dog people. That's fine; it is, in part, the reason for having fences (apart from the obvious problem that dogs have big mouths, full of pointy teeth, and can bite fairly hard). True story: on the third day of living in our house, our next-door neighbor wrote and deposited a letter in our mailbox, informing us of our dog's ill behavior of barking incessantly. He threatened us with legal action if we didn't rectify the barking situation immediately.

Frustrated, but ultimately on the wrong side of the law, we decided to install a dog door, so that Bailey could go outside to evacuate, but could remain inside. As we had hoped, our house proved to be sufficient in deadening her barking that we received no further complaints.

The point I'm trying to emphasize here is that, as dog owners, we are implictly responsible to our neighbors. We have a duty and an obligation to ensure that our dog doesn't pollute their air space with her barking, doesn't pollute their yards with her poop, and is properly fenced in lest she run out and bite someone.

And now we've come to it. My dog is not the point of this post. Your cat is.

Sure. Cats are funny. Cats are cute, sometimes. GIFs of cats are especially great. The problem is, however, cats are much more of a nuisance than they are a benefit to urban society. To be clear, I'm speaking of "outdoor" cats.

As a dog owner, I have an implicit responsibility to my neighborhood. Not so for cat owners, whose wards tend to spend as much time shitting in my garden and destroying my plants as they do in their owners yards.

Point blank, it is fucking disgusting to have to dig your cats' shit out of our garden beds. In fact, it's hard to believe it's even legal for you to have a cat in the city.

Indoor cats are cool, because they shit in their owners' houses, apartments, duplexes, and condos. Outdoor cats shit in my yard.

Indoor cats are cool, because they chew up their owners' house plants, which their owners are responsible for replacing. Outdoor cats chew up my fucking garden plants, and I am responsible for replacing them.

Indoor cats are cool, because they kill nothing, save the catnip rodent toy they love to hunt after and murder, each day, endlessly, like some kind of Matrix-come-to-life. Outdoor cats kill animals and leave them in my yard, as if in some kind of tribute to my generosity for letting them hunt my land.

The bottom line here is that if you own a cat, and let it roam the outdoors in an urban environment, you are being a dick. Straight up. You are not taking responsiblity for an animal that doesn't give a turd for its actions, and will take what it wants, from whom it wants, and will shit on that person after the fact.

If I haven't been clear thus far, let me be so: I don't hate cats. I just hate your cats, in my yard. But, because it's a cat and not a dog, it gets some sort of societal pass to do whatever the fuck it pleases.

If you own an outdoor cat, do yourself a favor, and go ask your neighbors how it pleases them to pick up cat poop from their yards. Ask them how many plants they've had to replace. Ask them what they did with the dead rodents found in their yard, adorning the Japanese Maple to which they so lovingly tend.

Ask them that, then ask yourself if your cat shouldn't be wearing a shock collar that strongly encourages it to not leave your yard.

I take care of my pet, and I protect her from doing stupid shit around the neighborhood. It's high time cat owners do the same.
